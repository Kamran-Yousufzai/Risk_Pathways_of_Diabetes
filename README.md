# 🩸 Risk Pathways of Diabetes
Diabetes is a growing global health challenge, leading to serious complications such as heart disease, kidney failure, vision loss, and reduced quality of life. Its rising prevalence places a heavy burden on healthcare systems and economies worldwide.

# 🎯 Diabetes Risk Factors
People most at risk of developing diabetes include those with obesity, sedentary lifestyles, poor dietary habits, or a family history of the disease.

⚠️ Health Vulnerability: High blood pressure, advancing age, and chronic stress.

🌍 Socioeconomic Factors: Limited access to healthcare or healthy food further heightens risk.

# 📊 Major Risk Variables for Diabetes
🧬 Genetic & Demographic Factors
🎂 Age: Risk significantly increases after age 40 to 60.

🌳 Family History: Having a parent or sibling with the condition raises diabetes risks.

🤰 Pregnancies: A higher count of pregnancies progressively increases the long-term risk of diabetes.

🩺 Clinical Biomarkers
🩸 Glucose: High levels (180–250 mg/dL) indicate a heavy risk for active diabetes.

💓 Blood Pressure: High ranges (101–120 diastolic) are heavily skewed toward high-risk diabetic profiles.

🧪 Cholesterol Level: Diabetics consistently show elevated levels (e.g., 200–250) which increases cardiovascular risks.

💉 Insulin Resistance Factors: Habits such as Smoking, Alcohol Intake, and poor Sleep Hours negatively affect insulin sensitivity.

⚖️ Physical & Lifestyle Metrics
📏 BMI (Body Mass Index): The obese range (31–40) shows clear diabetic dominance; while it may decline with age, the risk gap remains wide.

🏃 Physical Activity: Engaging in less than 150 minutes of weekly activity significantly raises vulnerability.

🧴 Skin Thickness: Often used in clinical data as an indicator of subcutaneous fat and metabolic health.

🥗 Diet Habits: A high intake of refined carbs, sugar, and processed foods triggers chronic spikes in blood sugar.

# 📉 Summary of Risk Pathways
Understanding these pathways is the first step toward prevention. By managing Blood Pressure, Glucose, and Lifestyle choices, the progression of the disease can be significantly slowed or even reversed.
